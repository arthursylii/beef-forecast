⚠ Key Seasonality Correction (v4.1): Calibrated from 36 observations per cut (2023–2026, 3yr × 12mo) · Jun 2026: LM_XB459/LM_XB460 actuals. Confirmed pattern: Rib/Loin SP/CO ratios peak DEC (+7.4%), secondary JUN (+1%), trough MAR (-4.2%) (holiday entertaining, prime rib season, restaurant bookings) and trough in Jul when live cattle prices are also elevated, compressing the ratio. The absolute $/cwt price does peak Jun–Aug, but the ratio to cutout and live cattle is highest in fall. Chuck/Round remain flat. Brisket peaks Mar–May (BBQ season onset). This correction affects all ratio-based forecast outputs.

Ratio methodology: SP/CO = (Sub-Primal $/cwt) ÷ (Choice Cutout $/cwt). SP/LC = (Sub-Primal $/cwt) ÷ (5-area Fed Steer $/cwt). Historical observations extracted from USDA LM_XB459/LM_XB859 at: May 2021, Oct 2021, Jun 2022, Mar 2023, May 2023, Oct 2024, Apr 2026 (n=1–5 per cut). Seasonal indices derived by computing monthly ratio deviations relative to the cross-year mean, then normalizing to avg=1.000. Cuts with only 1 observation use the parent primal's seasonal template. The forecast blends 70% direct SP/CO chain with 30% SP/Primal chain for stability. SP/LC = SP/CO × (Cutout/LC ratio), so seasonal patterns in SP/LC reflect both the cut's own seasonal variation and the cutout/live ratio seasonality.
🧪 ML Backtest — Walk-Forward Validation
For each held-out month, the model trains only on data before that point and predicts it — no future leakage. This tells you whether the ML forecast actually works, unlike in-sample R² which can look good even when overfitting.

How to read this: MAPE (Mean Absolute % Error) is the average size of the model's miss, as a percentage. Under ~3% is strong for a monthly commodity forecast; 3–7% is reasonable; above 10% means treat that cut's ML line as a rough guide only. Directional accuracy is how often the model correctly called whether price would go up or down month-over-month — 50% would be a coin flip. Cuts with thin trading volume or high week-to-week noise (e.g. specialty ribeye cuts) tend to backtest worse than high-volume, stable cuts (trim, ground beef, chuck).
